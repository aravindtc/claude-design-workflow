# Imagery and Cropping Patterns

## Core principle

Images are not decorations. They carry mood, proof, and emotional resonance.
Every image placement decision should answer: what does this image communicate, and does the crop support that?

## Cropping rules

**Preserve the subject** — the focal point of the image (face, product, gesture) must be visible and not clipped.

**Plan the crop for the text zone** — if text will overlay the image, crop to put the subject in the half that stays clear of text.

**Avoid cropping at joints** — do not crop human figures at the neck, elbow, knee, or wrist. Crop above or below joints.

**Maintain aspect ratio** — never resize an image by changing only width or height. Use resize + crop, not stretch.

## Placement strategies

### Full-bleed
Image fills the entire frame. Text must be protected by:
- A dark overlay (opacity 0.65–0.85) in the text zone
- A solid panel over part of the image
- Placing text over the inherently dark or light portion of the image

### Half-frame
Image occupies half the frame (left, right, top, or bottom). Text sits in the other half on a solid background.
Best for: clean split compositions, fashion, product.

### Contained/framed
Image sits inside a defined area — not full-bleed. Has visible margin from the frame edge.
Best for: editorial, premium, structured brand communication.

### Thumbnail/supporting
Image is smaller than the text block — supports rather than leads.
Best for: feature lists, testimonials, content posts where the copy is the hero.

## Image density vs text density

In a single frame, one should dominate:
- Image-led: image > 50% of frame area, minimal text
- Text-led: image < 30% of frame area, text is the dominant element
- Balanced split: image and text roughly equal, clear boundary between zones

Avoid compositions where neither the image nor the text clearly dominates — they create visual ambiguity.

## Image and color relationship

- If the image has warm tones, a warm accent palette feels cohesive
- If the image has cool or desaturated tones, a neutral or cool palette works
- Avoid accent colors that clash with dominant image colors (e.g., red accent on a red-heavy lifestyle photo)
